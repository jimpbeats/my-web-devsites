About Dubai

Dubai, a dazzling jewel in the Middle East, has transformed from a humble pearling and fishing settlement into a global metropolis renowned for its audacious architecture, luxury, and groundbreaking advancements. Founded in the early 18th century, Dubai's strategic location on the Persian Gulf allowed it to flourish as a trade hub, especially after declaring itself a free port in 1901.

The discovery of oil in the late 1960s accelerated its growth, but unlike many oil-rich nations, Dubai proactively diversified its economy into tourism, finance, trade, and technology. Under visionary leadership, it has consistently pushed the boundaries of what's possible, becoming a beacon of innovation and a hub for international business and leisure. Its commitment to smart city initiatives, sustainable development, and cutting-edge infrastructure positions it as a leader in the 21st century.

Dubai is a city of innovation and luxury, blending modern architecture with rich cultural heritage. Known for its skyscrapers, shopping malls, and desert adventures, Dubai offers a unique experience for every visitor.

From the bustling souks to the serene beaches, Dubai is a destination that captivates the senses.

Dubai's population is approximately 3.6 million (as of 2025), making it the most populous city in the United Arab Emirates. It is a melting pot of cultures, with over 200 nationalities living and working together, where expatriates make up about 85% of the population.

Dubai is located on the southeast coast of the Persian Gulf in the United Arab Emirates, at the crossroads of Europe, Asia, and Africa. It lies at approximately 25.2°N latitude and 55.3°E longitude, making it a strategic hub for global trade and travel.

Dubai has a hot desert climate with two main seasons: summer (May to October) with temperatures averaging 35–45°C (95–113°F), and winter (November to April) with milder temperatures of 20–25°C (68–77°F). Rainfall is scarce, occurring mainly in winter.

Culture & Heritage

Experience Dubai's rich cultural heritage through its traditional souks, museums, and historic districts like Al Fahidi. Learn about Bedouin traditions and the city's rapid transformation into a global metropolis.

Visit the Dubai Museum or take a stroll through the Alserkal Avenue for a blend of history and contemporary art.

Emirati culture is deeply rooted in its nomadic Bedouin past, with traditions like falconry, camel racing, and warm hospitality (Al Karam) where guests are offered Arabic coffee (Gahwa) and dates. Islamic values shape daily life and architecture, evident in the call to prayer and stunning mosques. Traditional attire, such as the Kandura and Abaya, reflects pride in identity, while crafts like Al Sadu weaving and Ayyala dances showcase vibrant cultural expressions.

Food & Dining

Dubai's culinary scene is a vibrant reflection of its cosmopolitan nature, offering an unparalleled gastronomic adventure for every palate. From traditional Emirati dishes that tell tales of the desert to Michelin-starred restaurants showcasing global cuisines, the city is a true food lover's paradise.

Try traditional Emirati dishes like Al Harees or indulge in fine dining at Burj Al Arab.
